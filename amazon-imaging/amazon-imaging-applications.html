Building Amazon's
Imaging Business

Amazon's seller imaging tools had gone without meaningful investment for over a decade. As the PM accountable for the full imaging product surface, the task was to navigate 12 stakeholder organizations, establish ownership of a fragmented ecosystem, and ship a suite of tools that would take image creation from a seller pain point to a scalable, compliant, high-volume platform.

The Work

Three initiatives, told in order: introducing Imaging Services into the legacy Seller Central experience, redesigning that experience end-to-end, and building a mobile imaging capability for the first time.

Initiative 1

Introducing Imaging Services into Seller Central

The Seller Central team owned the Manage My Images surface but had no bandwidth to invest in it. After a series of cross-team negotiations, the Imaging team secured ownership of the seller-facing UI intake — building the ordering flow directly into the existing interface as a modal, without requiring changes to the underlying Seller Central API. Sellers could now discover, configure, and order professional imaging services in one place for the first time.

Navigating the space Mapping the organizational landscape across Imaging Studios, Imaging Tech, Legal & PR, Billing & Finance, Seller Tools, and Selling Partners — a prerequisite before any product work could begin.
Defining the service offering Internal documentation explaining what Amazon Imaging is, how it works, and the shot goals available for sellers to order — covering white background images, environment sets, and lifestyle photography.
Image standards and service examples Style guides and lighting standards covering on-white product photography, talent shoots, and environment set categories (Office, Kitchen, Bedroom, Gym and more) — giving sellers clear expectations of what they'd receive.
Imaging Services ordering modal — first ingress point The initial ordering flow embedded directly into the legacy Manage My Images experience in Seller Central. Sellers could select image types, set quantities, add product notes, and place an order without leaving the platform.

Initiative 2

Redesigning the Seller Central Image Management Experience

Post-launch feedback made clear that the Imaging Services entry point was buried inside a legacy interface sellers found confusing and hard to navigate. The surrounding experience was owned by multiple teams with no shared UX standards, making the overall journey feel fractured. This became the mandate for a ground-up redesign of the image management experience in Seller Central — giving sellers a coherent, modern surface for uploading, managing, reviewing, and fixing their product imagery at scale.

BEFORE
A fragmented starting point The legacy Manage My Images experience in Seller Central — a scattered, desktop-only layout with no unified view, no batch actions, and no clear path to imaging services. Sellers had to navigate multiple disconnected pages to accomplish basic tasks.
AFTER
Batch uploads and proactive issue detection The redesigned dashboard introduced bulk image uploads with real-time processing feedback, alongside an inline notification system that surfaced compliance issues and image quality problems without sellers having to hunt for them.
AFTER
Your Image Library — one place for everything A unified, searchable library view giving sellers a complete picture of all their product images across their catalog. Sortable by ASIN, status, and last-updated date, with inline status indicators for compliance and live images.
AFTER
Image Details — full context in a single panel An expandable details modal surfacing all metadata for a selected image: compliance status, image type, dimensions, upload history, and direct actions to replace or reorder. Sellers no longer had to leave the page to understand the state of an image.
AFTER
Image Testing — A/B experimentation built in A self-serve A/B testing interface allowing sellers to pit two image variants against each other on a live listing. Sellers could define the test window, split traffic, and track click-through performance — a capability that previously required a vendor engagement.
AFTER
Imaging Services — integrated, not bolted on Rather than a separate modal buried in legacy flows, Imaging Services became a first-class tab inside the redesigned app. Sellers could order professional photography, track jobs in progress, and receive completed assets — all in the same experience where they managed their images.
AFTER
Self-service image settings and editing controls An updated Manage Images view with an inline settings panel, giving sellers granular control over image assignments, ordering, and display rules per ASIN — previously requiring a support ticket or manual intervention from the Seller Tools team.

Initiative 3

Mobile Imaging — Built Inside the Amazon Seller App

A parallel initiative developed in close partnership with the Amazon Seller App team in Santa Cruz. While the desktop redesign served professional sellers on Seller Central, millions of sellers globally were running their businesses entirely from mobile devices. The React Native imaging module gave them the ability to capture, auto-enhance, check compliance, and upload product images directly from their phone — a capability that had never existed before. Fifteen million uploads in year one validated both the demand and the quality of execution.

Product Photo Studio entry point The Product Photo Studio was surfaced as a dedicated entry in the Amazon Seller app navigation, giving sellers a clear, discoverable path to imaging tools from the mobile experience they already used daily.
Photo source — camera or library A simple action sheet letting sellers choose between taking a new photo on the spot or selecting an existing image from their device's photo library — keeping the entry point frictionless for both capture and upload workflows.
In-app camera with composition guidance A guided camera view with a composition grid to help sellers frame product shots correctly before capturing. Designed to reduce the number of retakes and improve first-attempt compliance rates for white-background requirements.
Photo editing — exposure, background, crop An inline editing suite covering the three most common post-capture fixes: exposure adjustment, AI-assisted background removal for white-background compliance, and manual crop. All editable before submission, without leaving the app.
Auto-crop for image standards compliance An intelligent crop tool with an auto-crop option that automatically applied Amazon's framing guidelines — product centred, correct padding, correct aspect ratio. Sellers could accept the suggestion or adjust manually with visual guides showing compliance boundaries.
Compliance review before submission A pre-submission review screen running automated compliance checks against Amazon's image standards — background, resolution, framing, and file type. All checks shown as passing, giving sellers confidence before the image goes live on their listing.

The Challenge

A Decade Without Investment

Amazon's imaging tools had been largely unchanged for more than ten years. Sellers struggled with outdated workflows, unclear compliance requirements, and no unified path to create, fix, or manage product images at scale. While the Seller Central team nominally owned the space, it had no place on their roadmap and no dedicated resource allocation. Through negotiation across engineering and product leadership, the Imaging team secured responsibility for the seller-facing UI intake — the front-end experience through which sellers access imaging services — while Seller Central retained ownership of the underlying API layer. This allowed the team to build and iterate on the product experience independently, without requiring a wholesale re-architecture of backend systems.

🕰️

No Investment for 10+ Years

The seller-facing imaging toolset had seen no meaningful product investment in over a decade. Sellers relied on workarounds and external tools that were not built to Amazon's compliance standards.

🏛️

12 Stakeholder Organizations

The imaging ecosystem spanned 12 distinct internal teams with overlapping mandates, competing priorities, and no shared roadmap. Coordinating alignment was a prerequisite to shipping anything at scale.

⚠️

Compliance Gaps at Scale

Sellers frequently submitted images that failed to meet Amazon's strict style and content guidelines. There were no in-product tools to help sellers self-diagnose or fix image defects before submission.

📱

Zero Mobile Capability

With millions of sellers operating from mobile devices globally, the absence of a mobile imaging tool created a major gap in accessibility. Sellers had no way to capture, edit, or upload images on the go.

The Platform

Three Surfaces, One Imaging Ecosystem

Rather than treating each channel as a separate product problem, the approach was to build a coherent imaging platform with shared services underneath and purpose-built experiences on top. Three surfaces were prioritized based on seller need, technical feasibility, and organizational readiness.

📱
Mobile Imaging
Built within the Amazon Seller App using React Native

The first mobile-native imaging experience for Amazon sellers, embedded directly into the existing Seller App. Sellers could capture, crop, enhance, and submit compliant product images from their phone.

The Approach

Navigating Complexity to Create Clarity

With 12 stakeholder organizations and no prior unified ownership, the first challenge was establishing shared understanding before any product decisions could be made. The approach prioritized alignment, then architecture, then delivery.

Discovery → Alignment → Shared Roadmap → Parallel Delivery → Integrated Platform
A five-phase model for building shared ownership across a fragmented stakeholder landscape
  • 1

    Cross-Organizational Discovery

    Ran structured discovery interviews across all 12 stakeholder organizations to map current-state capabilities, pain points, and opportunity areas. Documented overlapping mandates and surface dependencies to identify where coordination was most critical.

  • 2

    Seller Research and Needs Mapping

    Conducted qualitative research with sellers ranging from individual small businesses to large brand vendors. Mapped the full imaging journey from product photography through compliance review to understand where friction was highest and stakes were largest.

  • 3

    Unified Product Architecture

    Defined a platform architecture that separated shared imaging services from surface-specific experiences. This gave each stakeholder team a clear integration point while enabling consistent compliance logic across all seller-facing surfaces.

  • 4

    Parallel Mobile and Desktop Delivery

    Coordinated parallel development tracks for the mobile app (React Native, embedded in the Seller App) and the desktop redesign (Seller Central and Vendor Central). Maintained a single shared services layer beneath both tracks to ensure consistency.

  • 5

    Iterative Validation and Feedback Loops

    Established seller feedback mechanisms post-launch to track CSAT, image defect rates, and upload volumes. Used this data to drive prioritization of subsequent improvements and prove the case for continued investment in the platform.

Program Timeline

From Discovery to Platform Scale

The imaging platform was built in phases over two years, moving from organizational discovery through parallel product delivery to full platform integration and scale.

🔍
Early 2018
Established PM Ownership of Imaging
Took on accountability for the full Amazon seller imaging product surface. Began cross-organizational mapping across all 12 internal stakeholder teams to understand current-state capabilities and gaps.
🗺️
Mid 2018
Seller Research and Architecture Definition
Completed qualitative research with sellers and defined the unified platform architecture. Established shared services contracts across stakeholder teams and aligned on a sequenced delivery roadmap.
📱
Late 2018
Mobile Imaging Launched in Amazon Seller App
Shipped the first mobile imaging capability within the Amazon Seller App using React Native. Sellers could now capture, review, and submit compliant product images directly from their devices for the first time.
🖥️
Early 2019
Seller Central Desktop Redesign Shipped
Delivered the redesigned imaging experience within Seller Central, replacing decade-old workflows with a unified, compliance-aware interface. Defect detection and guided correction tools were included in the initial release.
🏢
Mid 2019
Vendor Central Imaging Overhaul Completed
Extended the new imaging experience to Vendor Central, bringing brand vendors onto the shared platform. Both Seller and Vendor Central now shared a consistent underlying services layer for compliance validation and defect management.
🚀
Late 2019 / 2020
Platform Scale Achieved: 200M Images per Week
The imaging platform reached 200M images processed per week, up from approximately 14M before the program began. Mobile uploads crossed 15M in the platform's first full year. CSAT settled at 4.5 out of 5 stars across all seller segments.

The Impact

A Platform Built to Last

In two years, Amazon's seller imaging capabilities went from an overlooked, outdated toolset to a high-throughput platform handling hundreds of millions of images per week. Sellers got the tools they needed to create and maintain compliant product imagery at any scale, from any device.

Organizational Alignment at Scale
Establishing clear product ownership across 12 stakeholder organizations was as significant as any individual product launch. A shared roadmap and API contracts created the foundation for sustainable investment in seller imaging for years beyond the original program.
Mobile-First Accessibility for Global Sellers
The mobile imaging launch opened Amazon's seller tools to a new category of operators who relied on smartphones as their primary business device. Fifteen million uploads in year one validated both the demand and the quality of the solution.
Compliance-by-Design
By embedding compliance guidance directly into the imaging workflow rather than treating it as a post-submission check, image defect rates dropped and seller confidence in the submission process increased. Defect resolution time fell by five days on average.
Scalable Infrastructure for Future Growth
The shared services layer built to support the initial three surfaces was architected to scale well beyond the program's original scope. At 200M images per week, the platform had headroom to support continued seller growth without requiring significant rearchitecture.
