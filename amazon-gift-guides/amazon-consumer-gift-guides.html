The Problem

Fragmented by Category, Failing by Design

By 2013, every Amazon retail category was producing its own gift guide independently. The result was a patchwork of inconsistent experiences - customers had to re-learn navigation patterns for every category, and production teams were duplicating enormous amounts of work with no shared assets or standards.

🗺️

Global Inconsistency

Multiple product categories across 5+ countries produced individual gift guides with no shared standards. Europe, Latin America, and North America each operated independently.

🔁

Duplicated Production

No shared assets, templates, or component libraries meant every team rebuilt the same patterns from scratch. Launches were slow and labor-intensive.

😕

Poor Customer Experience

Navigation patterns, filter designs, and content hierarchies varied by category. Customers expecting consistency were met with a different experience every time.

📉

No Strategic Foundation

UX, navigation, and visual design were treated as afterthoughts. High abandonment rates reflected the absence of intentional experience design.

Project Goals

What We Set Out to Achieve

🔍

Increase ASIN Discovery

Increase ASIN discovery and browsing opportunities by surfacing more relevant products through persona-driven navigation and a richer browsing architecture.

📱

Mobile Shopping Experience

Design a mobile shopping experience that matched the quality and usability of desktop, enabling customers to browse and purchase gift ideas on any device.

⚡

Dynamic ASIN Listings

Optimize ASIN listings to be dynamic, ensuring product cards reflected real-time availability, pricing, and relevance rather than static, manually curated snapshots.

🎁

Gift of the Day Placements

Increase Gift of the Day placements across the experience, creating consistent editorial moments that drove urgency and promoted featured products.

🌍

Global Widget Availability

Make widgets available globally, so every market could deploy the same standardized components without rebuilding from scratch for each locale.

🔄

Reusable Framework

Create a framework to reduce repeat work and optimize efficient execution globally, cutting production time and enabling consistent quality at scale.

The Old Experience

2013: Category Silos in Action

The Electronics and Toys & Games gift guides from 2013 illustrate the problem clearly - each with its own navigation structure, left-rail filters, visual hierarchy, and content strategy. There was nothing connecting these experiences.

2013 Electronics Gift Guide - left-rail navigation, category sidebar, no persona concept
2013 Toys & Games Gift Guide - Shop by Age sidebar, different layout paradigm entirely
"No thought had been applied to improving user experience, navigation and the volume of work that was generated as a result of lack of shared assets."

The Framework

A Standardized Product Architecture

The solution was to design a single, reusable gift guide product - one that could adapt to any retail category while delivering a consistent, persona-driven shopping experience. The new design introduced four core pillars.

🪟

Waterfall Grid

A masonry-style card layout replacing the traditional left-rail navigation. Maximum use of vertical space, consistent across all categories.

🧭

Persona Navigation

Icon-based top navigation linking to customer personas (e.g. "The Photographer," "The Gamer") replaced buried filter sidebars.

🎯

Hero Billboard

Full-width hero section at the top of every guide for editorial campaigns, promotions, and seasonal features - a consistent anchor point.

⚡

Quick View Details

Inline product detail panels on hover reduced navigation friction, keeping customers in the gift guide experience rather than bouncing to product pages.

The Design

From Wireframe to Deployed Across 9 Sites

The new design was validated through wireframes and then deployed at scale across 8 categories and 7 countries. Each instantiation used the same structural template, with category-specific persona icons and editorial content swapped in.
