How I conceived, designed, and drove adoption of a composite 0–100 KPI that unified customer experience measurement across RentSpree's entire product ecosystem, and became the north-star metric for the engineering organization.

The Challenge

We had data everywhere, but no unified quality signal

RentSpree operated with multiple data sources measuring different aspects of product performance in silos: customer satisfaction surveys, engineering incident reports, support ticket volumes, and feature funnel analytics. Leadership lacked a single, trustworthy number to answer the most fundamental question: "How good is our product right now?"

📊

Fragmented Metrics

CSAT, bug counts, ticket volumes, and funnel rates lived in separate dashboards. No one view connected them into an actionable whole.

📋

Survey Dependency

Existing quality proxies relied heavily on CSAT surveys: inherently lagging, low-volume, and disconnected from actual behavioral signals.

🎯

No Goal Alignment

Without a composite score, engineering, product, and design teams optimized for their own metrics with no shared definition of "quality."

⚖️

Incomparable Units

How do you compare a 4.1/5 CSAT to 15 bug tickets per 1,000 transactions to a 78% payout success rate? The data types made comparison impossible.

The Concept

PQS: A 360° view of customer experience quality

I conceived the Product Quality Score (PQS) as a single composite metric: a 0–100 score that aggregates multiple data sources to give leadership, product teams, and engineering a real-time, honest read on product quality across every touchpoint.

Unlike traditional survey-dependent metrics, PQS combines quantitative behavioral data with qualitative sentiment signals and technical reliability indicators, weighted and normalized into one number that anyone in the company can understand and act on.

The Methodology

How raw data becomes a single score

The core design challenge was making metrics with completely different units and scales comparable. I partnered with the data analytics team to develop a normalization methodology that converts every raw metric to a 0–100 scale before combining them with their assigned weights.

PQS = Average( Task Success Score, User Sentiment Score, Systems Reliability Score )
Each pillar score = weighted sum of its normalized sub-metric scores (0–100 each)
Domain PQS → Roll-up to Company PQS
Company PQS = Average(Start Domain PQS × 50%, Stay Domain PQS × 50%)
How normalization works

A normalized score converts metrics with different units and ranges into a common 0–100 scale, making it possible to compare performance across areas and combine multiple metrics into one weighted overall score.

  1. 1

    Collect raw data from each source

    Pull raw values: CSAT ratings, bug ticket counts, incident logs, payout timestamps, support call NLP scores, each in its native unit.

  2. 2

    Normalize to per-transaction basis

    Volume-sensitive metrics (bugs, incidents, tickets) are expressed per 1,000 paid transactions to eliminate seasonality bias as the business grows.

  3. 3

    Map to 0–100 scale using benchmark anchors

    Each metric is mapped to a 0–100 normalized score using historical min/max anchors and domain-specific thresholds defined with domain leads.

  4. 4

    Apply pillar weights and aggregate

    Normalized sub-metrics are multiplied by their assigned weights within each pillar, then the three pillar scores are averaged to the domain PQS.

  5. 5

    Roll up to company-wide PQS

    Domain PQS scores (Start and Stay) are averaged to the company-wide composite score, finalized mid-month following the prior month's data stabilization.

⚠️
Design note: data stabilization lag

Incoming data that informs PQS for a given month doesn't fully stabilize until the middle of the following month. I built this constraint explicitly into the OKR cadence: teams review the prior month's PQS in the monthly OKR sync rather than expecting real-time numbers, preserving data accuracy over false immediacy.

Adoption & Organizational Change

From concept to company north star: navigating real resistance

Getting PQS adopted wasn't straightforward. I deliberately involved cross-functional stakeholders throughout the design process, and when pushback came, I held firm on the principles that made the metric meaningful.

💡
Q3 2025 · Concept
Origin: bridging the data divide
I identified the need for a unified quality metric while reviewing fragmented dashboards. Worked with executive leadership and the data analytics team to define the pillar framework and normalization approach.
🔬
Oct 2025 · Development
Data model built & stakeholder review
The data analytics team built the spreadsheet-based normalization model. Product stakeholders across both domains were briefed. A proposal to remove Task Success metrics was raised and rejected. "We can't remove actual business performance from a quality score just because they are worried about roadmaps."
📊
Dec 2025 · Validation
First diagnostic use: tracing the Nov dip
The November PQS drop to 66.7 was traced directly to a bug ticket spike (~15 bugs in Start domain). This real-world diagnostic use case validated the framework's design and built organizational confidence.
🎯
Jan 2026 · OKR Formalization
Embedded as a 2026 Company OKR
PQS was formally adopted as a key result in RentSpree's 2026 company OKR framework. Domain leads across product and engineering set their own PQS sub-targets, creating direct accountability at the team level.
🚀
Early 2026 · Engineering Adoption
Software engineering adopts PQS across all squads
Incoming engineering leadership adopted PQS as the primary quality KPI for the software engineering organization, marking the first time engineering had a unified quality benchmark. PQS is now tracked alongside velocity across all squads, giving engineering teams a shared, actionable signal tied directly to customer experience.

Leadership challenge navigated

Product managers proposed removing Task Success from the score, concerned about accountability. I pushed back clearly: quality measurement must include whether the feature actually works for the customer, not just how it feels or how stable the infrastructure is. Retaining this pillar preserved the metric's integrity and ultimately made it more trusted by leadership.

Cross-functional ownership model

PQS deliberately has no single owner. I designed it as a shared accountability mechanism: domain trio leads (Design, Engineering, Product Management) all own their domain's score together. This required explicit alignment on planning practices and roadmap time allocation to address quality signals.
